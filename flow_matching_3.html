Part 3 — Training, Loss & Optimal Transport
// From intractable integrals to a simple training loss · Why straight lines win
The Problem — Why We Can't Just Compute the Full Flow
The intractability issue

From Part 2 we know the marginal vector field is an integral over all data points. In theory, you could compute this and train a neural network to predict it. In practice: you can't compute that integral. It's over the entire data distribution.

The original flow matching loss — intractable
ℒ_FM(θ) = 𝔼_{t, p_t(x)} ‖ v_θ(t, x) - u_t(x) ‖² where u_t(x) = ∫ u_t(x|x₁) · p_t(x|x₁)/p_t(x) · q(x₁) dx₁ ↑ integral over entire dataset — INTRACTABLE to compute directly
🚫 The marginal vector field u_t(x) requires integrating over all possible data points. With a real dataset of millions of images, you can't do this exactly. You need a smarter approach.
THE BREAKTHROUGH

The paper proves that the Conditional Flow Matching (CFM) loss has the same gradients as the original intractable loss.

This means training with CFM gives you the same optimal neural network as if you could compute the full integral!

Why gradients = parameters

Two loss functions have the same gradients w.r.t. θ means they differ by a constant independent of θ. If ∂ℒ/∂θ is the same, gradient descent finds the same minimum — so you get the same trained model.

The Conditional Flow Matching (CFM) Loss
CFM loss — tractable & simple
The conditional flow matching objective
ℒ_CFM(θ) = 𝔼_{t, q(x₁), p_0(x₀)} ‖ v_θ(t, φ_t(x₀)) - u_t(φ_t(x₀) | x₁) ‖² t ← sample a random time t ∈ [0, 1] uniformly q(x₁) ← sample one data point x₁ from your dataset p_0(x₀) ← sample one source point x₀ from 𝒩(0, I) φ_t(x₀) ← move x₀ along the conditional path to time t u_t(·|x₁) ← the conditional vector field (tractable! just derivatives of μ_t and σ_t)
🎯 Instead of matching the full marginal vector field (intractable), match the conditional vector field for a single sampled pair (x₀, x₁). Sample a new pair each training step. Average across many steps → same result as the full integral, guaranteed by the theorem.
One training step — what actually happens
1
Sample a data point x₁ from your dataset Pick one random training example — one image, one audio clip, etc.
2
Sample a source point x₀ ~ 𝒩(0, I) Sample random Gaussian noise. This is your starting point.
3
Sample a random time t ∈ [0, 1] Pick a random intermediate time. The loss is averaged over all t.
4
Compute the intermediate point: x_t = φ_t(x₀) Where should x₀ be at time t, if it's travelling toward x₁? For the optimal transport choice: x_t = (1-t)·x₀ + t·x₁ — a linear interpolation.
5
Compute the target vector: u_t(x_t | x₁) For the straight-line OT path: target = x₁ - x₀. This is just the direction from source to target. Crucially: it does not depend on t.
6
Backpropagate: minimize ‖ v_θ(t, x_t) - (x₁ - x₀) ‖² The neural net at position x_t at time t should predict the direction x₁ - x₀. MSE loss. Simple, clean, tractable.
One training sample — what the net sees
Making it Concrete — Gaussian Conditional Paths
📐 So far everything is general: we said "define a conditional path from x₀ to x₁" but didn't say how. Now the paper makes a specific choice: let all intermediate distributions be Gaussians parameterized by a time-dependent mean μ_t and standard deviation σ_t. This subsumes both diffusion models and the optimal transport choice.
Gaussian conditional path definition
p_t(x | x₁) = 𝒩(x | μ_t(x₁), σ_t²I) ← a Gaussian at every time step t with time-dependent mean and std dev μ_t(x₁) ← how the center moves over time σ_t ← how the spread changes over time Boundary conditions: μ_0 = 0, σ_0 = 1 ← matches source 𝒩(0,I) μ_1 = x₁, σ_1 = σ_min ← centered on data point

The actual path could be wiggly or straight — the only constraint is that at t=0 you match the source Gaussian, and at t=1 you're centered tightly on the data point x₁.

Conditional vector field — derived analytically
u_t(x | x₁) = (σ̇_t/σ_t)(x - μ_t(x₁)) + μ̇_t(x₁) σ̇_t = d(σ_t)/dt ← derivative of std dev μ̇_t = d(μ_t)/dt ← derivative of mean

Because the path is Gaussian at every step, you can derive the vector field analytically. You know exactly what direction each point should move — no approximation needed.

The push-forward trick
φ_t(x₀) = σ_t(x₁)·x₀ + μ_t(x₁) ← scale x₀ by σ_t, shift by μ_t to get the point at time t on the path

Simple linear interpolation along the Gaussian path. You can compute x_t without running an ODE solver.

Two Specific Choices — Diffusion vs Optimal Transport

The Gaussian framework lets you recover both diffusion models (with complicated curved paths) and the new optimal transport approach (with straight lines). The difference is entirely in the choice of μ_t and σ_t.

Option A — Diffusion Conditional Paths
μ_t(x₁) = α_t · x₁ ← nonlinear schedule σ_t = √(1 - α_t²) ← noise schedule u_t(x|x₁) = [σ̇_t/σ_t · (x - α_t·x₁) + α̇_t·x₁] ↑ involves integral functions, complex
⚠ This recovers the exact diffusion objective. But notice: at t=1 you get a division by zero — the equation is undefined at the boundary! This corresponds to the t→∞ problem in standard diffusion.
Diffusion: mean and std dev over time (nonlinear)
Option B — Optimal Transport (Straight Lines) ✦ RECOMMENDED
μ_t(x₁) = t · x₁ ← linear! σ_t = 1 - (1 - σ_min) · t ← linear! φ_t(x₀) = (1 - (1-σ_min)·t)·x₀ + t·x₁ ≈ (1-t)·x₀ + t·x₁ ← simple linear interp u_t(x|x₁) = x₁ - (1-σ_min)·x₀ ≈ x₁ - x₀ ← CONSTANT: no dependence on t!
✅ The vector field doesn't depend on time t. At every moment, every point just says "go toward x₁". Straight line. Clean. Efficient. Defined at all t ∈ [0, 1].
OT: mean and std dev over time (both linear)
What The Neural Network Actually Learns
The net learns a weighted directional field

At training time, for each sample (x₀, x₁, t), the network is taught: "at position x_t at time t, move toward x₁ (direction: x₁ − x₀)." But here's the key: many different data points x₁ could be the target for this position.

Multiple data points pulling on a query point — the network learns their average direction
🧠 The neural network learns to output a density-weighted average direction toward all nearby data points. Points close in the current flow distribution contribute more. This is how the knowledge of the entire dataset gets encoded into the vector field predictor.
The remarkable simplification

For the optimal transport (straight-line) choice, the target vector field simplifies dramatically:

Target at training time: u_t(x_t | x₁) = x₁ - x₀ That's it. No t anywhere. The direction from source to target. Constant throughout the trajectory.
✨ This is the cleanest possible training signal: "wherever you are at whatever time, just point toward your target data sample." The network generalises this to unseen data by learning the average over all training pairs.
Full OT training loop (pseudocode)
for each training step:
  x₁ = sample_dataset()
  x₀ = sample_gaussian()
  t = uniform(0, 1)

  x_t = (1-t)*x₀ + t*x₁
  target = x₁ - x₀

  pred = neural_net(t, x_t)
  loss = mse(pred, target)
  loss.backward()
  optimizer.step()
The Complete Picture
From data to generation — putting it all together
The full flow matching framework end-to-end
🏆 Why flow matching wins: OT straight-line paths mean the neural network's job is as simple as possible — "predict the direction to the data point." Straighter paths → fewer ODE steps at inference → faster generation. Used in Stable Diffusion 3, Flux, Qwen3-TTS.
⊃ Diffusion is a special case: Choose a specific nonlinear μ_t and σ_t and you recover exactly the diffusion objective. Flow matching is strictly more general — you can choose better paths than diffusion's noising process.
🎯 The key theorem: CFM loss (one pair per step) and the full FM loss (integral over all data) have the same gradients → same trained model. This is what makes the whole approach tractable.
